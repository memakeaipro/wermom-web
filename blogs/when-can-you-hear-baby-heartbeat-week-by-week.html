When Can You Hear Baby's Heartbeat? Week-by-Week Guide

PregnancyBy Wermom Medical TeamUpdated May 20266 min read

You can usually hear your baby's heartbeat for the first time at around 6 weeks of pregnancy via transvaginal ultrasound, and at 10 to 12 weeks with a handheld fetal Doppler at your prenatal visit. The exact week depends on the device used, your body type, and the position of your uterus.

This guide walks through the full week-by-week timeline, what to expect at each milestone, and what it really means if the heartbeat is harder to hear than expected. All information is based on guidelines from the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG), the American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP), and the U.S. Food and Drug Administration.

Quick Answer: First visual flicker on transvaginal ultrasound — 5.5 to 6 weeks. First audible heartbeat on transvaginal ultrasound — 6.5 to 7 weeks. First Doppler heartbeat at a routine prenatal visit — 10 to 12 weeks. Stethoscope-audible — 18 to 20 weeks.

Week-by-Week Timeline

Weeks 5 to 6: First Electrical Activity

The fetal heart starts beating at around 5.5 to 6 weeks of gestation. At this stage it is not really a heart yet — it is a tube of cardiac cells that have begun to contract rhythmically. Most parents will not be able to hear anything yet, but a transvaginal ultrasound at 6 weeks can sometimes visualize the flicker on screen even before it is audible.

Weeks 6 to 7: First Audible Heartbeat (Transvaginal)

By the end of week 6 and into week 7, the heartbeat is consistently detectable on transvaginal ultrasound in healthy pregnancies. According to ACOG, absence of cardiac activity by 7 weeks with a confirmed gestational age may suggest a non-viable pregnancy — but a follow-up scan is always recommended before any conclusion is drawn, since dating is often off by several days.

Typical heart rate at this stage: 90 to 110 beats per minute (bpm), increasing daily.

Weeks 8 to 9: Heartbeat Speeds Up

By week 9, the heart rate typically climbs to around 140 to 170 bpm — fast and strong. Abdominal ultrasound may now be able to detect it, though transvaginal is still more reliable for some body types.

Weeks 10 to 12: First Doppler Heartbeat at Your Prenatal Visit

This is the milestone most parents remember. At your routine 10 to 12-week appointment, your provider uses a handheld Doppler — a small wand that emits ultrasound waves and translates them into the familiar whooshing sound. By 12 weeks, the uterus has risen above the pubic bone, making the heartbeat much easier to isolate.

Weeks 13 to 19: Reliable Doppler Every Visit

Through the second trimester, the Doppler picks up the heartbeat at every visit. Normal range stabilizes at 110 to 160 bpm. Many parents describe the rhythm as galloping horses.

Weeks 20+: Stethoscope-Audible

From around 18 to 20 weeks onward, an experienced clinician can sometimes hear the heartbeat through a standard stethoscope or a Pinard horn (an old-fashioned wooden listening tube still used by midwives in many parts of the world). This is also when many parents first feel baby's movements.

How Is the Heartbeat Detected at Each Stage?

Three tools cover almost every prenatal visit:

  • Transvaginal ultrasound — earliest and most sensitive. Used from about week 5.5 onward when the heartbeat is too faint for abdominal scans. Briefly uncomfortable but completely safe.
  • Abdominal ultrasound — standard for the 12-week dating scan and the 20-week anatomy scan. Detects heartbeat reliably from about week 8 to 9.
  • Fetal Doppler — handheld device used at routine prenatal visits from week 10 to 12 onward. Translates blood flow into audible sound.

Should You Buy a Home Fetal Doppler?

This is one of the most common questions we get at Wermom — and the honest answer is: probably not. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and ACOG both advise against routine home Doppler use, for one main reason: false reassurance is more dangerous than no information at all.

Home Dopplers are designed to be operated by trained professionals who can distinguish between the fetal heartbeat (110 to 160 bpm), the maternal pulse (60 to 100 bpm), the placental whoosh, and the uterine artery flow. Without that training, parents commonly mistake their own pulse for the baby's. A "found a heartbeat" reading that is actually the maternal pulse can delay urgent care if the baby is genuinely in trouble.

If you are anxious between visits, a better option is to:

  • Track kick counts from week 28 onward
  • Use the Wermom app to log symptoms and contact your provider quickly
  • Call your OB triage line — every clinic has one — if anything feels off

What to Do if No Heartbeat Is Detected

Hearing the words "I can't find a heartbeat" is terrifying. Take a breath. Here is what usually happens next:

  1. Before 9 weeks: The most common explanation is a dating error. Your provider will rescan in 7 to 14 days. In a large clinical study published in Obstetrics & Gynecology, more than 30% of "no heartbeat" findings before 7 weeks turned out to be viable pregnancies on rescan.
  2. 10 to 12 weeks at a Doppler exam: The baby may simply be in an awkward position. Providers often try a different angle, ask you to shift, or move you to ultrasound for confirmation. A missed Doppler reading at this stage is common and rarely indicates a problem on its own.
  3. After 12 weeks at routine visits: If the Doppler does not pick up the heartbeat, an ultrasound will follow the same day. This is precautionary — most cases resolve with a clear heartbeat on the screen.

Common Questions Parents Ask

What is the earliest week you can hear a baby's heartbeat?

5.5 to 6 weeks via transvaginal ultrasound. By 6.5 weeks, the heartbeat is consistently visible in over 95% of healthy pregnancies according to ACOG.

When can you hear baby's heartbeat with a Doppler?

10 to 12 weeks in a clinical setting. Before 10 weeks the uterus sits low in the pelvis and the heartbeat is hard to isolate from the maternal pulse.

Is it safe to use a home Doppler?

Physically yes, but the FDA and ACOG advise against routine home use because parents often misread the maternal pulse as the baby's heartbeat — which can delay care if something is wrong.

What is a normal fetal heart rate?

110 to 160 bpm from about 10 weeks onward. Earlier in pregnancy (6 to 7 weeks) it is slower, around 90 to 110 bpm.

What does it mean if no heartbeat is detected at 6 weeks?

The most common cause is a dating error — the pregnancy is earlier than estimated. Your provider will usually rescan in 7 to 14 days.

Can you hear a heartbeat with a stethoscope?

Generally not until 18 to 20 weeks, and only with a quiet room and an experienced listener. Doppler or Pinard horn are far more reliable.

When can a partner or family hear the heartbeat?

At the routine 12-week prenatal visit, when your provider uses a Doppler. Some clinics also offer recordings.

Key Takeaways

  • First visible heartbeat: 6 weeks (transvaginal ultrasound flicker)
  • First Doppler heartbeat at your prenatal visit: 10 to 12 weeks
  • Normal fetal heart rate from week 10 onward: 110 to 160 bpm
  • Skip the home Doppler — false reassurance is the real risk
  • No heartbeat at 6 weeks usually means a dating error, not loss — rescan in 7 to 14 days
