When to Worry About Baby Development: Red Flags by Age

Every parent worries about whether their baby is developing normally. This guide distinguishes genuine concerns from normal variation. Wide developmental ranges exist—knowing them prevents unnecessary anxiety while catching real issues.

3 Months: What's Normal vs. Red Flags

Normal at 3 Months

✓ Smiling socially (smiling at faces, not just gas)

✓ Following objects with eyes (not perfect tracking, but looking at moving things)

✓ Making cooing sounds (not talking, just vowel sounds)

✓ Lifting head briefly (during tummy time, not sustained)

✓ Grasping reflexively (holding things put in hand)

Red Flags (Discuss with Pediatrician)

⚠ Not making eye contact (avoiding looking at faces)

⚠ Not smiling responsively (no social smile by 3 months)

⚠ Not tracking objects (doesn't follow movement)

⚠ Excessive stiffness or floppiness (abnormal muscle tone)

⚠ Asymmetrical movement (one side moves more than other)

6 Months: Expected Skills vs. Concerns

Normal at 6 Months

✓ Rolling both directions (or at least trying)

✓ Babbling (ba-ba-ba, da-da-da sounds)

✓ Sitting with support (not independently yet)

✓ Reaching for objects (with both hands or one)

✓ Showing emotions (laughing, showing interest/boredom)

Red Flags

⚠ Not babbling (no consonant-vowel sounds)

⚠ Not reaching for objects (hands not engaged)

⚠ Extreme stiffness or floppiness (beyond muscle tone variation)

⚠ Not making eye contact or responding to name (by 6 months, should respond to familiar voice)

⚠ No interest in people (withdrawn, not engaging socially)

12 Months: Walking & Language Milestones

Normal at 12 Months

✓ Cruising furniture (or starting to; not walking independently required)

✓ 2-5 words (even just "dada," "mama," "hi")

✓ Pointing (pointing at objects, people)

✓ Playing peek-a-boo (understanding object permanence)

✓ Showing emotions (clapping, excited, shy)

✓ Eating finger foods (some coordination)

Red Flags (Discuss with Pediatrician)

⚠ No babbling with consonants ("ba," "da," "ga")

⚠ Not responding to name (should turn when hearing name)

⚠ No pointing or gesturing (not communicating non-verbally)

⚠ Not understanding simple requests ("where's your shoe?")

⚠ No interest in other children (withdrawn, not watching or engaging)

⚠ Extreme difficulty with transitions (beyond typical toddler stubbornness)

18 Months: Language Explosion Time

Normal at 18 Months

✓ 10-50 words (huge range is normal; could be 5 words or 100)

✓ Following 2-step directions ("get your shoe and come here")

✓ Climbing, running clumsily (not gracefully)

✓ Showing empathy (offering comfort, showing concern)

✓ Pretend play starting (feeding doll, talking on phone)

Red Flags

⚠ Fewer than 5 words (language delay possible)

⚠ Not understanding words (doesn't respond to "where's mommy?" etc.)

⚠ Extreme difficulty with separation (panic, not just sadness)

⚠ Not interested in playing with other children (completely withdrawn)

⚠ Repetitive movements without purpose (spinning, lining up toys repetitively)

2 Years: Communication & Social Leap

Normal at 24 Months

✓ 50-250 words (huge range normal)

✓ Combining 2 words ("more milk," "mommy up")

✓ Running, climbing, kicking ball (clumsily)

✓ Playing alongside other children (not necessarily with them)

✓ Following 2-3 step directions (some confusion is normal)

✓ Identifying body parts (pointing to nose, eyes)

Red Flags

⚠ Fewer than 50 words total (significant delay)

⚠ Not combining words by 2.5 years (language disorder possible)

⚠ Losing skills previously had (regression always concerning)

⚠ Extreme anxiety or aggression (beyond normal toddler behavior)

⚠ No pretend play (not engaging imaginatively)

3 Years: Talking & Playing

Normal at 3 Years

✓ Speaking in 3-4 word sentences ("I want more juice")

✓ Speech 75% understandable to strangers (some unclear words normal)

✓ Pedaling tricycle (not fast, but moving pedals)

✓ Playing imaginatively (pretend play with toys, dolls, friends)

✓ Separating from parent at preschool (after adjustment period)

Red Flags

⚠ Speech less than 50% understandable (significant delay)

⚠ Not using 3-word phrases (language development delay)

⚠ No interest in other children (preferring isolation)

⚠ Difficulty with transitions/changes (extreme distress at routine changes)

⚠ Aggressive behavior regularly (hitting, biting frequently)

General Red Flags at Any Age

Loss of skills: If baby was doing something (babbling, making eye contact, reaching) and stops, that's concerning regardless of age.

Asymmetrical movement: One side of body consistently more active/weak than other suggests neurological concern.

Extreme rigidity or floppiness: Unusual muscle tone at any age warrants evaluation.

No response to name by 12 months: Should respond to familiar voice by 6 months, consistently by 12 months.

No pointing by 12-14 months: Pointing is important communication milestone.

No interest in people: Complete withdrawal from social interaction at any age concerning.

When to Seek Evaluation

Schedule developmental screening if you notice: Child not meeting 2+ milestones for age, losing skills previously had, extreme differences from peers, behaviors causing concern (aggression, anxiety, rigidity).

Important: Early intervention (birth to 3) is free in most US areas. Getting evaluation answers early is worth it. Early intervention is most effective before age 3.

Normal variation vs. disorder: One child talking less is normal. One child not responding to name, showing no social interest, not using gestures—that combination warrants screening.

The Bottom Line

Wide ranges are normal. Huge variation in development exists and most variation resolves. BUT if you're genuinely concerned (after researching normal range), request screening. It's free. It's non-invasive. And if there IS a concern, early intervention is most effective.

Trust your instinct. Anxious worrying is normal. But specific observation of skills baby isn't showing is worth investigating.
