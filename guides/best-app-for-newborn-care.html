Newborn Care Essentials in the First Weeks

Feeding (first 4 weeks): Newborns typically feed 8-12 times per 24 hours. Breastfed babies should see wet diapers increase daily (1 per day of life, increasing to 6+ by day 5-6). Formula-fed babies typically consume 2-3 ounces per feeding, increasing gradually. Track feeds to identify patterns and ensure adequate intake.

Sleep (first 4 weeks): Newborns sleep 16-18 hours per day in fragmented blocks. No "sleep schedule" yet—that develops by 3-4 months. Focus on safe sleep (firm surface, back sleeping, room-sharing without bed-sharing).

Diapers: Track wet diapers (should increase daily, 6+ by day 5-6) and poops (varies by feeding type). Color changes: meconium (black/green) day 1-3, transitional (greenish) day 4-5, mature (yellow for breastfed, tan for formula) by day 5+.

Health monitoring: Weight loss is normal (up to 10% of birth weight acceptable, should regain by day 10-14). Watch for jaundice (yellowing skin/eyes peaking day 3-5), fever (call immediately), or difficulty feeding.

First Newborn Checkups

Before hospital discharge (24-48 hours): Bilirubin check, metabolic screening, hearing screening, physical exam, weight check.

3-5 days (often home visit or office visit): Weight check critical—especially for breastfeeding. Feeding assessment. Jaundice evaluation.

2 weeks: Weight check to confirm adequate growth and feeding.

2 months: First well-child visit with vaccines, physical exam, developmental assessment.

Bring tracking data to appointments: Wet/dirty diaper counts, feeding duration/volume, sleep patterns, any concerns. This helps your pediatrician assess if baby is doing well.

Red Flags: When to Contact Pediatrician Immediately

Feeding: Not passing meconium by 24 hours, fewer than 5-6 wet diapers by day 5-6, painful breastfeeding after day 3, difficulty latching that doesn't improve.

Sleep/Lethargy: Unusually difficult to wake, doesn't eat when aroused, extreme sleepiness beyond normal newborn sleep.

Temperature: Fever (rectal temp >100.4°F) in newborn is medical emergency—call immediately.

Jaundice: Yellow color spreading to body/limbs (not just face), high bilirubin readings from screening.

Breathing: Gasping, grunting, retractions (skin pulling in with each breath), bluish color.

Other: Vomiting (not spit-up), diarrhea (frequent, watery), rash spreading rapidly, swelling/tenderness.

Newborn Care Tracking Tips

When to start: Day 1 at hospital. Recording baseline feeding/diaper patterns helps you and your pediatrician assess baby's health quickly.

How detailed?: Time, duration (breastfeeding), amount (bottle feeding), diaper output, and any concerns. Simple entries are fine.

How long?: Intensive tracking helpful first 4-6 weeks. By 8 weeks, patterns are established and you can track less frequently if desired.

Partner involvement: Use shared tracking app to coordinate night feedings/wake-ups and ensure partner understands baby's patterns.
