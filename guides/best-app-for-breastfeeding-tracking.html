Breastfeeding Tracking Basics

What to track: Duration on each breast, latch quality (comfortable, painful), pain level (0-10), any supply or breast health concerns, baby's wet/dirty diapers (confirms adequate intake).

First week patterns: Expect 8-12 feedings per 24 hours in first 2 weeks. Duration varies (10-45 minutes depending on latch efficiency). Longer feeds early on are normal; duration decreases as feeding efficiency improves.

How to know baby is getting milk: Wet diapers increase daily (1 per day of life up to day 5, then 6+ per day). Poops change from meconium to mature stool by day 5. Weight loss is normal (up to 10% acceptable), should regain by day 10-14.

Supply concerns: Low frequency/duration, not hearing swallows, few wet diapers, slow weight gain. Oversupply: leaking, engorgement, very frequent feeds. Bring tracking data to lactation consultant for assessment.

Pumping Tracking for Working & Exclusively Pumping Parents

Track for exclusively pumping: Time of day, duration, volume per breast, storage location. This helps optimize supply and plan bottle prep.

Track for working mothers: Pumping times at work, volumes, supply trends (increasing, stable, declining). Use data to adjust frequency if needed.

Milk storage guidelines: Room temperature (77°F or colder): 4 hours. Refrigerator (40°F): 4 days. Freezer (0°F or colder): 6-12 months. Use oldest milk first (FIFO method).

Supply prediction: Average pumping yield is 10-15 oz per day, but normal range is 10-45 oz depending on demand. Tracking shows if supply is stable or declining as you introduce bottles/formula.

Common Breastfeeding Issues & Tracking

Sore nipples: Normal first week, should improve. Red/cracked/bleeding nipples or pain beyond 5-7 days suggests latch issue—seek lactation help. Track pain level and any visual changes.

Mastitis: Blocked duct or infection. Signs: red, swollen, painful breast, fever, chills. Prevention: frequent draining (nursing/pumping), avoid tight bras, massage. Treatment: urgent—contact provider immediately if symptoms develop.

Thrush: Yeast infection. Signs: white patches on baby's mouth or mother's breast, burning pain during/after feeding. Treatment: antifungal cream/medication. Can be transmitted between baby and mother during feeding.

Engorgement: Early milk coming in (days 3-5) causes uncomfortable swelling. Normal and temporary (resolves in 24-48 hours with frequent feeding). Relief: nursing, warm compresses, gentle massage, cold compresses after nursing.

FAQ: Breastfeeding & Tracking

Q: Should I track every feeding?
A: First 2 weeks: yes, to establish patterns and confirm adequate intake. After that: 3-4 days per week is enough to spot trends without fatigue.

Q: How do I know if I have enough milk?
A: Look at baby's output (wet/dirty diapers) and weight gain, not your breast fullness. Tracking helps you see the big picture. Lactation consultant can assess by weighing baby before/after feeding.

Q: Can I pump instead of nursing?
A: Yes. Exclusive pumping is a valid choice. You'll need to track volumes carefully to maintain supply and ensure baby gets adequate milk.

Q: When should I introduce a bottle?
A: Typically weeks 3-4 once breastfeeding is established (if exclusively breastfeeding). Some mothers introduce earlier if returning to work. Discuss with lactation consultant based on your situation.
