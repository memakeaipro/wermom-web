Pregnancy Development by Trimester

First Trimester (Weeks 1-13): Fertilization, implantation, cell division into three layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) that will become all baby's organs. By week 8, baby has beating heart, brain activity. By week 13, baby is recognizably human-shaped (2.7 inches long). Most miscarriages occur first trimester; risk drops significantly after.

Second Trimester (Weeks 14-26): Rapid growth and organ development. By week 20, baby can hear, swallow, and move detectably. Ultrasound confirms anatomy. By week 26, baby is viable outside womb with intensive care (though survival rates improve significantly with each additional week). Mother feels movement, weight gain increases.

Third Trimester (Weeks 27-40): Final growth and maturation. Baby moving frequently, responding to light and sound. Brain development critical. By week 37, full term. By week 40, baby weighs 7-8 lbs, 19-21 inches long, ready for birth. Labor can begin anytime after 37 weeks (full term).

Key Pregnancy Milestones by Week

Week 4-5: Positive pregnancy test, HCG levels rising.

Week 8: First ultrasound, heartbeat visible, nausea typically peaks.

Week 12-13: First trimester screening, nuchal translucency ultrasound.

Week 16-18: Quad screen blood test (second trimester screening).

Week 18-20: Anatomy ultrasound (detailed fetal anatomy assessment).

Week 20: Mother feels baby move (quickening).

Week 24: Glucose tolerance screening (gestational diabetes check).

Week 28: Third trimester begins, RhoGAM if Rh-negative.

Week 28-32: Growth ultrasound (assessing baby size and growth rate).

Week 35-36: Group B Strep screening, position assessment (head down is ideal).

Week 37+: Non-stress tests if high-risk, labor monitoring if past due date.

Pregnancy Symptom by Week Progression

Early pregnancy (weeks 4-12): Nausea, vomiting, fatigue, breast tenderness, heightened smell, food aversions, mood swings. These typically improve after first trimester.

Mid-pregnancy (weeks 13-26): Energy returns, nausea resolves, baby movement feels amazing, skin may glow or break out, stretch marks may appear, occasional Braxton-Hicks contractions (painless), heartburn begins.

Late pregnancy (weeks 27-40): Growing discomfort, back pain, frequent urination, swelling, difficulty sleeping, stronger Braxton-Hicks, pelvic pressure, lightening (baby drops lower, breathing easier but more pelvic pressure).

Accuracy of Due Dates & Week Counting

Important note: Due dates are estimates, not predictions. Only 3-5% of babies arrive exactly on due date. Due date is calculated as 40 weeks from last menstrual period (LMP) or adjusted based on early ultrasound dating (more accurate if done first trimester).

Full term: 37-40 weeks. Babies born at 37+ weeks are full-term. Early term (37-38 weeks) has slightly higher risk than term (39-40 weeks) but is considered full term for most purposes.

Going past due: Induction typically recommended at 42 weeks (2 weeks past due date) due to placental insufficiency risk. Many providers encourage induction at 41 weeks.

Using Week-by-Week Information Effectively

Don't panic over probabilities: Apps quote risks like "1 in 5000 chance of X condition." Remember: 4999 out of 5000 babies don't have X. Screening tests show risk; positive result doesn't mean condition exists.

Use for communication: Bring week-by-week app information to prenatal visits. Show provider which developmental milestones you're tracking to start conversations about concerns.

Remember individual variation: Baby development is a range. Your baby may follow exact timeline or be ahead/behind in different systems. Ultrasound dating is most accurate first trimester; dating becomes less accurate later.
