Baby Meal Planning by Age: Timeline & Readiness

4-5 months: Show interest in food, sit upright with support, lost tongue-thrust reflex. Not ready—wait.

6 months (readiness check): Sits upright without support, reaches for food, brings objects to mouth, lost tongue-thrust. Ready for first foods (single-ingredient purees).

7-9 months: Multi-ingredient foods, thicker textures, soft finger foods (toast strips, soft fruits), progressing chewing.

10-12 months: Mostly family foods, mashed or cut small, progressing to chopped table foods, self-feeding with fingers and spoon.

12-24 months: Family meals (cut appropriately), expanding variety, developing preferences, transitioning off baby purees.

2-3 years: Full family meals, established preferences, exploring textures, snacking patterns emerge.

Allergen Introduction: What the Science Says

The 2015 Learning Early About Peanut allergy (LEAP) study found early allergen introduction (around 6 months, after solids started) may actually reduce allergy risk compared to delayed introduction. Modern recommendations: introduce common allergens early, frequently, and not in isolation.

Common allergens to introduce early: Peanuts (peanut butter), eggs, milk, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, wheat, sesame, soy.

How to introduce: Start with small amount mixed into familiar food (e.g., peanut butter in sweet potato). Introduce one allergen per week to identify reactions. Do this during the day when you're home, not before travel.

Signs of allergic reaction: Immediate (rare): vomiting, anaphylaxis. Delayed (more common): rash, vomiting 2+ hours later, diarrhea. Contact pediatrician if any concerns.

Choking Hazards to Avoid

Do NOT offer: Whole nuts, grapes, cherry tomatoes (cut lengthwise into quarters), popcorn, hard candy, raw carrots, whole olives, hot dogs (until age 4), marshmallows, chunks of cheese, large dollops of nut butter.

Safe finger foods: Soft cooked vegetables, ripe fruit (mashed or cut appropriately), scrambled eggs, soft bread, yogurt, cheese cubes, ground meat, beans (mashed).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: When should I use baby food pouches vs. homemade?
A: Both are fine. Pouches are convenient; homemade lets you control ingredients and costs less. Many families do both.

Q: How much should a 6-month-old eat?
A: Start with 1-2 teaspoons, progressing to 2-3 tablespoons per meal by month 2-3 of solids. Follow baby's hunger cues, not portions.

Q: Should I introduce foods in a specific order?
A: No. Old advice said vegetables before fruits; new evidence says order doesn't matter. Introduce whatever you want in any order.

Q: What about dairy-free or vegan diets for babies?
A: Possible with planning. Breast milk or formula is essential first year. Need fortified alternatives and attention to vitamin D, B12, iron, and protein. Work with pediatrician or dietitian.
